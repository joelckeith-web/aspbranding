# Joel Keith — Voice Profile

*Source material: 2 YouTube transcripts totaling ~17,700 words. Extracted 2026-04-23.*

**Important source note:** Both source files turned out to be interviews, not the advertised solo monologue. `apDaXcGh8Tc.txt` is Joel on Drew B Wilson's "Call the Damn Leads" (not the titled monologue "We Relied on One Lead Source"). `B8Xplp3uC1w.txt` is Joel on Richard Walsh's "Sharpen the Spear." Joel's speech is identified by context: answers following questions, first-person ASP references ("our agency," "my partner," "I"), and marketing-operator framing. Richard and Drew's speech is excluded where attribution is clear. No pure solo monologue was available, so Section 5 (metaphors) and Section 11 (energy) draw on whatever rhythm survives the interview format.

## Section 1: Signature Phrases & Openers

Verified recurring phrases pulled from Joel's speech across both transcripts:

1. **"Um, you know..."** — His most frequent verbal filler when shifting into a point. Appears dozens of times. Not a tic to eliminate — it's part of his measured, thinking-out-loud cadence.
2. **"Like any good entrepreneur..."** — Used twice self-deprecatingly when describing selling something before he knew how to deliver it.
3. **"So it's funny you bring that up..."** — His default warm transition into a story the interviewer has teed up.
4. **"The honest truth is..."** / **"let's call it what it is"** — Appears when he's about to be blunt about a trade (e.g., "they like the mechanics of it, right? Like that's call it call it what it is").
5. **"Having a come to Jesus meeting"** — Used to describe the partner confrontation. Classic Joel-ism for a hard internal conversation.
6. **"We were way over our head, but we were figuring this out as we go like any good entrepreneur, right?"** — Near-verbatim twice.
7. **"Hey, here's..."** — Recurring opener when he role-plays dialogue or pitches a solution ("Hey, uh, as a leader, I'm noticing these KPIs...").
8. **"At the very least..."** — Used when describing fallback positions / minimum-viable behaviors.
9. **"Um, so..."** as a full restart — when he's re-anchoring after a tangent.
10. **"That in itself..."** — Connector when isolating one variable from a larger system.
11. **"Not just [X], but [Y]"** — Frequent structural move ("Not just effective with the strategy, but also effective costwise").
12. **"To an extent..."** — Softens a concession before pivoting ("to an extent, you have to have a process, right?").
13. **"Like, actually [verb]..."** — Emphatic contrast with surface-level behavior ("actually sitting down and understanding, okay, why are they not hitting their sales").
14. **"Regardless of..."** — Used when stating a universal principle ("we can't rely on this one thing regardless of how good it is").
15. **"From a [X] standpoint"** — His favorite framing preposition. "From a marketing standpoint," "from a leadership perspective," "from a cost standpoint," "from a business standpoint." Appears ~15+ times.
16. **"Kind of..."** — Hedge word he leans on heavily. Functions as a softener, not a weakness signal.
17. **"And then..."** — Primary forward-motion connector in a sequence of steps.

## Section 2: How Joel Opens Topics / Hooks Readers

1. **Story-first, principle-second.** When given an open prompt, he reaches for a specific business moment: "about five or six years ago, I had a marketing agency..." He grounds the abstract in a dated, specific memory before extracting the lesson.
2. **Concede the interviewer's framing, then extend it.** "Yeah, so it's funny you bring that up because that's exactly what happened..." He validates the question, then uses it as a launch pad rather than redirecting.
3. **Name the systemic angle up front.** Opens his origin story with: "my my sales story comes from more of a systemic issue inside of our business." He signals the category (systemic, process, structural) before the narrative.
4. **Role-play the dialogue.** When introducing a topic he'll illustrate by quoting the exchange: "in this conversation, it's like, 'hey, this is we're kind of at a critical point here...'" The reader hears the meeting, not a summary.
5. **Start with the tension, not the resolution.** "Sales stopped. Uh, the contracts were not getting signed. What leads we did have weren't going through." He lets the problem sit before moving to what he did about it.

## Section 3: How Joel Transitions Between Ideas

1. **"Um, so..."** — His workhorse transition. Almost every major pivot opens with it.
2. **"And then..."** — Linear, sequential. Used when stacking actions or escalating cost/effort tiers.
3. **"And I think..."** — Flags a shift from description to interpretation/lesson. Often immediately followed by what the experience taught him.
4. **"On top of that..."** — Used to pile on a second causal factor or stressor (his HVAC customer empathy riff uses this repeatedly).
5. **"So really..."** — Resets to the core takeaway after a digression. ("So really it was okay if you haven't even put any effort in the past 90 days...")
6. **Restating the interviewer's phrase back.** Picks up Richard's or Drew's exact words and builds the next paragraph off them — a collaborative, not combative, handoff.

---

## Section 4: How Joel Closes / Lands Points

1. **Recap as a tiered list.** Lands big points by climbing a revenue-tier ladder: "500K to 700K... 1 million and more... when you get to 3 to 5..." Each tier gets a prescription.
2. **"And that's the thing..."** / **"that's kind of..."** — Soft landing phrases that signal he's wrapping.
3. **Future-consequence close.** Wraps advice by pointing at what happens if you don't do the thing: "you just wasted money getting in front of somebody who is never going to buy your product."
4. **Offering the alternative path.** Almost never closes with a single prescription — he'll add "or... here's phase one. Let's figure out your foundations." He leaves the client a stepdown option.
5. **Emotional acknowledgment as the punchline.** His HVAC riff lands on: "they're probably stressed out. All of these things are happening... How can I position myself or what can I do to ease their mind at all?" The point is the human moment, not the tactic.

## Section 5: Analogies, Metaphors, Mental Models

Verified from the transcripts:

1. **"The only ship that doesn't sail is a partnership."** — He quotes this approvingly from Richard but owns the idea: "I have a thing about partnerships."
2. **"Money guy isn't a partner, that's an investor."** — A definitional distinction he rides as a mental model: partners must bring a unique skillset, not capital.
3. **"Rich people buy time"** — Cited from Dan Martell's *Buy Back Your Time* as a mindset shift that changed how he thinks about delegation.
4. **"You don't rise to the occasion, you fall to your highest level of preparation."** — Quoted as a frame for why he coaches client sales teams.
5. **"Silent fractional CMO"** — Self-description for clients who don't want involvement: "I'll be your silent fractional CMO."
6. **"Social media celebrity kind of thing"** — His metaphor for why local trades owners should be on-camera: familiarity as a differentiator against PE-backed brands with bigger budgets.
7. **"Shoehorn them into that one thing"** — His critique of single-service agencies.
8. **"Lick dirt for a while"** — His phrase for the early grind in business.
9. **"Begging for scraps"** — Contrast phrase for desperation-mode client acquisition.
10. **Revenue-barrier ladder (0–$300K hustle, $1M marketing-expert, $3–5M systems-and-processes, $5–10M fractional CMO).** This is Joel's fingerprint mental model — he returns to it unprompted. It's effectively his framework for diagnosing a trades business.
11. **The capacitor/$550 HVAC scenario.** Full imagined customer journey used to teach empathy-in-sales. This is his longest, most worked-in illustration.

Signal is thin on pure metaphor because both sources are interviews where Joel is operating in explanation mode. The revenue-ladder and the HVAC empathy walk-through are the two most load-bearing models.

## Section 6: Sentence Structure Patterns

1. **Long, clause-stacked sentences joined by "uh" and "um."** Joel does not speak in crisp fragments. He builds compound sentences with embedded hedges: "Um, so having that relationship building uh poised so well for the referral base."
2. **Self-interrupting restarts.** He'll begin a sentence, notice a better framing, and restart mid-thought: "Well, that's not always the case. Sometimes that is the case, but we have to..."
3. **Role-played dialogue instead of indirect speech.** He almost always quotes ("It's like, 'Hey guys, revenue is down...'" rather than "we told them revenue was down").
4. **Rhetorical questions as teaching structure.** "What's your age targeting? What is your income range?" Strings of 2–4 questions to force the listener to feel the gap.
5. **Tiered enumeration with "okay."** "Okay, now you need a project manager... Okay, that one to three million is..." "Okay" functions as a mile marker, not a filler.
6. **Parenthetical asides dropped in plainly.** "(although we do service fulfillment)" — casual, not formatted.
7. **Front-loads the situation, back-loads the judgment.** Almost never opens a sentence with the verdict. He sets the scene, then tells you how to read it.

## Section 7: Vocabulary Preferences

**Gravitates toward:**
- **Operator/finance terms used casually:** MRR, KPI, ROI, ICP, AOV, fractional CMO, ad spend, backlinks, AEO/GEO, LLM search, close rate, billables, service fulfillment.
- **"Foundations," "strategy," "systems and processes," "scale," "diversify," "channels"** — the operator's vocabulary.
- **"The trades," "home service professionals," "local businesses"** — his preferred customer nouns. He does NOT say "SMB" or "prospects."
- **"Dude," "man," "guys"** — informal address markers that appear throughout.
- **"Absolutely"** — his preferred emphatic ("absolutely important," "absolutely like," "absolutely is still the biggest roadblock").
- **"Comprehensive," "intake meeting," "deck," "audit"** — the process-vocabulary of his sales motion.

**Conspicuously avoids / rare:**
- MBA jargon like "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb), "alignment," "stakeholder."
- Growth-hacker / influencer-speak ("hack," "crush it," "10x," "ninja").
- Overclaiming adverbs like "literally," "revolutionary," "game-changing."
- Cursing is minimal — one "damn" in context of the podcast title; zero f-bombs.
- Technical marketing deep-jargon (he uses terms like AEO/GEO but always in the same breath as the plain-English gloss: "optimizing for LLM search").

**Distinctive mixed register:** plain-spoken ("odd guys because they like the mechanics of it") directly adjacent to operator-precise ("7% of your revenue toward marketing, depending on geographic area and competition"). He moves between registers without signaling the shift.

## Section 8: How Joel Handles Credibility / Self-Reference

- **Understated on his own wins.** "We're back on track just in the past 18 months moving past the 45K MRR month mark." Reports numbers flatly, no flourish.
- **Self-deprecating about failures.** Opens his biggest story with the partner who "didn't believe in our product and I have not been selling it for 90 days" — told as a systemic failure he shared ownership of, not a villain story.
- **Credits others for frameworks.** Names Dan Martell. Credits his wife for teaching him the camera. Credits his partner for the ad knowledge.
- **"My partner and I joke all the time that we'd be a lot more well off if we didn't have integrity."** — Signature move: he lands a credibility claim as a joke.
- **Client stories are told to illuminate the client, not Joel.** The home inspector story ends on the client's revenue growth, not Joel's strategy.
- **Never name-drops clients by name.** Refers to them by trade: "one of my clients is a home inspector," "an HVAC company that switched from a really big well-known agency."
- **Owns ASP plainly:** "our agency," "CEO and managing partner of a digital marketing agency called ASP." No titles puffery.
- **Will admit what he doesn't do:** "I never ran the paid ad campaigns. Always had someone to help me with that."

## Section 9: How Joel Frames Problems

- **Systemic, not personal.** Attributes failures to missing systems, checks, or diversified channels — not bad actors. The partner who stopped selling is framed as a symptom of over-centralization, not a betrayal.
- **Matter-of-fact tone.** "We went down to less than 10K in revenue per month from doing almost 80K months." No melodrama.
- **Empathetic when the problem is the client's end-customer.** The HVAC homeowner gets a full interior-monologue treatment: PTO, pets, the $67,000 estimate, compounding stress.
- **Direct but not harsh about agency peers.** "A lot of marketing charlatans out there" — names the pattern without naming firms. "Marketers are definitely good at sales" is his gentle indictment.
- **Names the trap before the fix.** Consistently describes the failure mode ("one BNI chapter and that was it") before the correction.
- **Uses "we" when the problem was his company's, "a lot of people" when it's a general pattern.** Careful with pronouns.

---

## Section 10: How Joel Frames Solutions

- **Conditional and tiered, not prescriptive.** "If you're at 500 to 700K... hire a smaller guy. If you're at a million and more... that's the time to start considering an agency." Almost never "do X."
- **Leads with diagnosis, not prescription.** The TOPA audit / competitor analysis comes before any recommendation. "Bring the findings back — this is before we even sign a contract."
- **Offers the downgraded alternative.** "If we're not ready to spend that, here's phase one. Let's figure out your foundations."
- **Coaches rather than commands.** "Let's change your language when you're talking to a customer" — first-person plural, inclusive.
- **Grounds every solution in the client's ROI, not vanity metrics.** "I can't deposit likes in my bank account" — his explicit rejection of marketing theater.
- **Always leaves an out.** Ends prescriptions with "or" branches. Rarely closes off options.

## Section 11: Energy / Pacing / Emotion

Caveat: interview format limits the signal. Still:

- **Measured, not emphatic.** Joel rarely spikes. He doesn't shout, doesn't deliver zingers. Richard and Drew are the emphatic ones; Joel holds a steady mid-tempo.
- **Warmest when talking about client wins.** The home inspector anecdote is the closest he gets to visible pleasure: "That feels good to me. Not because that like my team and I have created this for them but that he's making that much more money."
- **Driest when calling out bad-faith competitors.** Delivers "we'd be a lot more well off if we didn't have integrity" flat — it reads funnier because of the deadpan.
- **Speeds up when inside a mental model.** The revenue-ladder walk-through has visibly faster clause-stacking than his origin story. When he's in his framework, he flows.
- **Slows and softens on empathy content.** The HVAC homeowner scenario slows down — more pauses, more "you know," more hedges. The rhythm itself performs the empathy he's advocating.
- **No anger markers.** Even when describing being burned (the hostage website / theme-license story) he stays in explanatory mode.

## Section 12: Things Joel Would Never Say

Inferred guardrails:

- "Crush it," "10x your revenue," "hack your growth," "ninja," "rockstar."
- "Literally" as an intensifier. (He uses "literally" rarely and only literally.)
- "Synergy," "leverage" as a verb, "circle back," "at the end of the day," "move the needle."
- "Trust me" — his credibility comes from process and results, not assertion.
- Single-sentence hot takes with no setup. Joel earns his verdicts.
- Name-dropping clients or gurus as status signals (he'll cite Dan Martell's book by title, not to flex).
- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "disrupt."
- Absolute prescriptions without a conditional ("You must..." / "Never..."). He always carves an exception or a cheaper path.
- Disparaging other agencies by name or with heat. The sharpest he gets is "charlatans" and "shoehorn."
- Profanity beyond the mildest ("damn," and only in the host's branded phrase).
- Dancing-on-TikTok / performative-founder energy. He explicitly distances from it ("I don't want to dance on camera like on TikTok").

## Section 13: A Voice-Writing Rubric

A draft sounds like Joel if it:

1. **Opens with a concrete situation or dated memory,** not an abstract claim. ("About five or six years ago..." beats "Many agencies struggle with...")
2. **Uses the revenue-tier ladder or a similar stepped framework** when prescribing. Different advice for different stages, always.
3. **Names the failure mode before the fix.** The trap comes first; the correction comes second.
4. **Grounds every recommendation in ROI or client-outcome, never in vanity metrics.** Impressions, likes, and follower counts are explicitly devalued.
5. **Role-plays dialogue** at least once per piece — quoted exchanges, not summarized ones.
6. **Drops in operator vocabulary (MRR, ICP, AOV, fractional CMO) without defining it** for peers, or with a quick plain-English gloss when writing for trades owners.
7. **Hedges with "kind of," "you know," "um-so"–style softeners** in moderation. Don't scrub them all; they're part of the texture. But don't overdo — a written piece shouldn't read like a transcript.
8. **Offers a downgraded alternative** at the end of any prescription ("or, here's phase one...").
9. **Credits others** (a book, a partner, a client) at least once rather than posturing as the sole authority.
10. **Lands on the human moment, not the tactic.** The HVAC capacitor scene isn't about upselling — it's about the homeowner's stress. Blog posts should do the same.
